# Caption — fig2_trap_design_space

**Figure 2 | Sulfur content opens a composition-tunable charge-trap design space
beyond conventional dielectrics.**
**a**, Origin of deep traps. Homolytic cleavage of a backbone S–S bond yields two
sulfur radicals that act as deep Coulomb traps; the polymer's low dielectric
constant produces a wide, deep Coulomb well (poor screening) from which charge is
retained, in contrast to the shallow, narrow well of a conventional high-ε
dielectric, from which charge readily detraps.
**b**, Conceptual trap design space spanned by trap-distribution breadth
(horizontal, exponent n) and charge retention / resistivity (vertical, ρ).
Conventional dielectrics (PI, PDMS, PET) cluster in a shallow, leaky corner,
whereas the sulfur series S60→S85 traces a composition-tuned trajectory into a
region beyond the reach of conventional dielectrics. Positions are schematic, not
measured coordinates.
**c**, Kinetic and magnitude signatures (schematic). (left) The transient current
follows a power law I(t) ∝ t^(−n); its log–log slope is steeper for sulfur (larger
n, a broad dispersive trap distribution) than for a conventional low-n dielectric,
relative to a Debye reference. (right) The transition-zone resistivity ρ rises with
sulfur content and lies far above the conventional-dielectric band. Curves are
idealized and number-free; quantitative plots appear in Fig. 3.
